Timeline of LGBTQ Revolution

"CRUCIAL BECAUSE IT SOUNDED THE RALLY FOR THE MOVEMENT." — Lillian Faderman, historian, The Gay Revolution

  1. 1700s “Homosocial”

    The term “homosocial” was used to describe publicly-accepted close relationships between two same-sex friends.

  2. 1700s "Queer"

    The term “queer” changed from meaning “quaint” to “bad”.

  3. 1776-1870

    Women transgressed gender identities and norms by enlisting in military as men. Joseph Lobdell/Lucy Ann Lobdell, a well-known marksperson, was born as a woman and lived as a man. The institution of slavery, in its dehumanizing function, removed gendering markers on social roles.

  4. 1800s "Boston marriage"

    The term "Boston marriage" was used to refer to close relationships between women; the extent to which these relationships were romantic and/or sexual is unknown for most friendships.

  5. 1848-1919 Women's Rights

    Beginning in the 1840s with Amelia Bloomer, the women's rights movement included dress reform to free women from the bondage of dress.

  6. 1860-1900 Cross-Dressing

    States and municipalities banned cross-dressing.

  7. 1869 First drag balls

    The first drag balls began at Harlem's Hamilton Lodge in 1869. Ball culture, drag ball culture, or house-ballroom culture describe an underground LGBTQ+ subculture that originated in the United States, in which people "walk" (i.e., compete) for trophies, prizes, and glory at events known as balls. Ball participants are mainly young Black and Latinx members of the LGBTQ+ community. Attendees can spectate, dance, vogue, and/or walk in one or more of the numerous drag categories that are designed to emulate other genders and social classes, while offering a sort of escape from reality. This culture extends beyond the extravagant events as most participants in ball culture belong to groups known as "houses" that mimic a family structure and offer similar support.

  8. 1869 "Homosexual"

    Hungarian Karl-Maria Kertbeny developed the term “homosexual” which was often used in medicine and law throughout the early 1900s; today the term has an offensive connotation.

  9. 1872 Herculine Barbin’s memoir

    Parts of Herculine Barbin’s memoir were published. She was born intersex in France but assigned first as a female and then later a male.

  10. 1892 "Heterosexual"

    The word heterosexual is first used in Chicago Medical Recorder.

  11. 1895 “Gross indecency”

    Oscar Wilde was found guilty of “gross indecency” and served two years in prison.

  12. 1897 "Lesbian"

    Havelock Ellis, an English physician, first used the term “lesbian”, which refers to the Isle of Lesbos in Greece, home of Sappho (a Greek poet who wrote about love between women).

  13. 1908 The Intersexes

    Edward Prime-Stevenson’s The Intersexes was published.

  14. 1914

    Magnus Hirschfeld’s Homosexuality in Men and Women was published.

  15. 1916

    Despite their growing popularity, drag balls were deemed illegal and immoral by mainstream society. A moral reform organization known as the Committee of Fourteen periodically investigated the balls. In 1916, the committee released a report detailing the scandalous behavior they witnessed. The report described a scene filled with "phenomenal" "male perverts" in expensive frocks and wigs, looking like women. The committee later released 130 reports describing its visits, demanding that such perversion must desist.

  16. 1918 Earl Lind

    Earl Lind (also known as Ralph Werther and Jennie June), self-described androgyne, published "Authobiolgraphy of an Androgyne". Lind later published "The Female Impersonators." These works made androgynes visible and documented their suffering.

  17. 1919

    Dr. Magnus Hirschfield's research and compilations of data documented the presence of sexual minorities. He introduced the concepts of "transvestite" and "sexual intermediaries."

  18. 1920s

    The term “queer” began to be used offensively towards the LGBTQ community.

  19. 1924

    The Society for Human Rights, the first gay rights organization in the U.S., was formed by Henry Gerber.

  20. 1930 Production Code

    The Hollywood Production Code was enacted, which banned references to homosexuality among other rules.

  21. 1930s “Dyke” & “faggot”

    “Dyke” and “faggot” became slurs that were used in the U.S.

  22. 1930s "Gay"

    “Gay” became a term used within the LGBTQ community.

  23. 1940 Major Griffin-Gracy

    Miss Major Griffin-Gracy was born on October 25, 1940. Miss Major is a trans woman activist and community leader for transgender rights, particularly for trans women of color. She has participated in activism for a wide range of causes throughout her lifetime, including the 1969 Stonewall riots in New York City. She currently serves as the Executive Director for the Transgender Gender Variant Intersex Justice Project, which aims to assist transgender persons who are disproportionately incarcerated under the prison-industrial complex.

  24. 1945 Marsha P. Johnson

    Marsha P. Johnson was born on August, 24, 1945. Marsha was an American gay liberation activist and self-identified drag queen, known as an outspoken advocate for gay rights. She was one of the prominent figures in the Stonewall Riots, a founding member of the Gay Liberation Front, and co-founded S.T.A.R. (Street Transvestite Action Revolutionaries), alongside close friend Sylvia Rivera.

  25. 1950 The Lavender Scare

    The Lavender Scare began with the firing of 91 people believed to be homosexual from the U.S. State Department.

  26. 1950

    Pauli Murray authored "States' Laws on Race and Color", bringing together important legal ideas of race and gender. She identified as a masculine woman and was California's first black deupty attorney general.

  27. 1950s The postwar era

    The postwar era is an important time for Queer organizing. New organizations such as Mattachine, Daughters of Bilitis and ONE, Inc. Each also had a journal to reach and connect their members, many of who joined these organizations secretly.

  28. 1951 Sylvia Rivera

    Sylvia Rivera was born on July 2, 1951. Sylvia was a Latina American gay liberation and transgender rights activist significant in the LGBTQ+ history of New York City and of the United States as a whole. Rivera was a founding member of the Gay Liberation Front and co-founded Street Transvestite Action Revolutionaries (STAR) with Marsha P. Johnson, a group dedicated to helping homeless young drag queens, gay kids, and trans women.

  29. 1952

    The American Psychiatric Association described homosexuality as a “sociopathic personality disturbance” in its manual.

  30. 1952-1953 Homosexual bans

    The Immigration and Nationality Act bans homosexual immigrants; Executive Order 10450 makes homosexuality grounds for dismissal from federal employment.

  31. 1955

    Daughters of Bilitis were formed to offer a space for lesbian women.

  32. 1961 Hose and Heels Club

    Virginia Prince organized the Hose and Heels Club in Los Angeles as a kick-off for political organizing for cross-dressing men.

  33. 1964-1969 Picketting

    In cities across the country, small groups of queer activists picket anti-gay policies. Sites of pickets include the White House, the Pentagon, Independence Hall, United Nations, Cooper Union. Picketers target the criminalization of homosexuals and other denials of rights and equality.

  34. April 21, 1966

    A sip-in was held at Julius’ Bar against attempts to revoke the bar’s liquor license because it served the LGBTQ community.

  35. March, 1970

    A bar frequented by the LGBTQ community, the Snake Pit, was raided by police.

  36. June 28, 1970

    The first Pride Parade took place in New York City.

  37. 1970 STAR

    Marsha P. Johnson and Sylvia Rivera started the Street Transvestite Action Revolutionaries (STAR).

  38. 1972

    Numerous openly gay and lesbian people elected to political office.

  39. 1972 House of LaBeija

    The House of LaBeija is a prominent drag family founded by Crystal LaBeija and Lottie LaBeija in 1972. Crystal and Lottie established the House of LaBeija in response to racial prejudice in the New York ballroom scene. Their first event was called "Crystal & Lottie LaBeija presents the first annual House of Labeija Ball at Up the Downstairs Case on West 115th Street & 5th Avenue in Harlem, NY." This event was thought to be the birth of house culture as it is known today. Houses serve as alternative families, primarily for gay, gender nonconforming and transgender youth and others who feel ostracized from conventional support systems.

  40. 1972-1976

    The Mattachine Society opened an office at 59 Christopher Street.

  41. 1973 Oscar Wilde Bookshop

    The first gay bookstore, the Oscar Wilde Bookshop, opened at 15 Christopher St (it was open previously on a different street).

  42. 1978

    The original gay pride flag was created by artist Gilbert Baker and flew at the San Francisco Gay Freedom Day Parade celebration on June 25, 1978.

  43. 1979 Gay Liberation

    "Gay Liberation", a sculpture by George Segal was commissioned to commemorate the Stonewall Riots. It was moved to its current location in Sheridan Square in 1992.

  44. 1982 House of Xtravaganza

    In 1982, House of Xtravaganza is formed by Hector Valle (1960-1985), a gay man of Puerto Rican descent as the first all-Latinx house in NYC, a predominantly Black ball room scene. The House of Xtravaganza is one of the most publicly recognized “houses” to emerge from the New York City underground ballroom scene and among the longest continually active. House of Xtravaganza members and the collective group are recognized for their cultural influence in the areas of dance, music, visual arts, nightlife, fashion, and community activism. House of Xtravaganza members continue to be featured in popular media and travel the world as ambassadors of voguing and the ballroom scene。

  45. 1990 Paris Is Burning

    Paris Is Burning, an American documentary film directed by Jennie Livingston, was released in 1990. Filmed in the mid-to-late 1980s, it chronicled the ball culture of New York City and the Black, Latinx, gay, and transgender communities involved in it. Some critics considered the film to be an invaluable documentary of the end of the "Golden Age" of New York City drag balls, and an exploration of race, class, gender, and sexuality in America.

  46. 1990

    The LGBTQ community reclaimed “queer” but the term can be used offensively today depending on the context.

  47. 1999

    The Transgender Pride Flag was created by American trans woman Monica Helms in 1999, and was first shown at a pride parade in Phoenix, Arizona, United States in 2000. The flag represents the transgender community and consists of five horizontal stripes: two light blue, two pink, and one white in the center.

  48. 2001

    The Netherlands became the first country to legalize same-sex marriage.

  49. 2003

    Belgium became the second country to legalize same-sex marriage.

  50. 2004

    Same-sex marriage became legal in Massachusetts.

  51. 2008

    Connecticut legalized same-sex marriage.

  52. 2009 Harvey Milk Day

    May 22nd became “Harvey Milk Day” in California.

  53. 2017

    Philadelphia flew an updated pride flag at its first annual Pride Kick-Off at City Hall. The new pride flag added black and brown stripes to the top of the traditional six-color rainbow layout to highlight queer people of color's activism in the LGBTQ+ community.

  54. 2018

    Portland-based designer Daniel Quasar created a new pride flag to include the colors of the trans flag, as well as black and brown stripes added by the pride flag redesign from Philadelphia to increase representation and inclusion in the flag design.
